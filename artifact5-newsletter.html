The Deal

A Historic Agreement

Constellation Energy · Microsoft · NRC · March 2026

In September 2024, Microsoft and Constellation Energy signed a landmark 20-year Power Purchase Agreement — Constellation's largest-ever — to restart Three Mile Island Unit 1 as the Crane Clean Energy Center. The deal delivers 835 MW of carbon-free nuclear power to Microsoft's mid-Atlantic AI data centres for two full decades.

The agreement directly supports Microsoft's commitment to become carbon negative by 2030 — a goal made harder by explosive AI infrastructure growth. It locks in guaranteed around-the-clock clean energy at a fixed price, providing long-term cost certainty alongside the environmental benefit.

"Nuclear plants are the only energy sources that can consistently deliver carbon-free, 24/7 power."

— Joe Dominguez, CEO, Constellation Energy
Background

From Infamy to Revival

Three Mile Island is perhaps America's most recognised nuclear address. In 1979, Unit 2 suffered a partial core meltdown — the most serious accident in US nuclear history. Unit 1, an entirely separate reactor, was unaffected and continued operating safely for another four decades.

Unit 1 closed in 2019 — not for safety reasons, but because cheap natural gas made it uneconomical. The explosive growth of AI transformed that calculus entirely: hyperscale data centres need always-on, carbon-free power that renewables alone cannot reliably provide.

The plant was rechristened the Crane Clean Energy Center in honour of former Constellation CEO Chris Crane, who championed nuclear as a clean baseload solution long before AI made the argument mainstream.

Why Nuclear?

The AI Energy Crisis

Microsoft's emissions rose 23.4% in fiscal year 2024, driven almost entirely by data centre expansion. Generative AI is extraordinarily power-hungry — training and running large language models demands uninterrupted electricity that strains conventional grid thinking.

Wind and solar, while vital, are intermittent. Data centres cannot tolerate gaps. Nuclear provides dense, reliable, carbon-free power every hour of every day regardless of weather or season — exactly what AI infrastructure requires.

Microsoft has pledged to match 100% of its consumption with carbon-free energy on an hourly basis by 2030. Certificates do not satisfy that standard. Only physical clean baseload generation does — and Three Mile Island Unit 1, fully licensed and sitting idle, was the most pragmatic solution available.

Why This Deal Works

  • No new-build delays or cost overruns

  • Existing NRC licence framework in place

Industry Context — Big Tech's Nuclear Race

Amazon secures 1.9 GW

Amazon secured up to 1.9 GW from Talen Energy's Susquehanna plant — the largest single nuclear offtake deal after the Microsoft agreement, signalling that cloud hyperscalers are now treating nuclear procurement as a competitive necessity.

Google targets SMRs

Google contracted Kairos Power for 500 MW of Small Modular Reactor output — the first major corporate commitment to next-generation nuclear technology, betting that SMRs will be commercially viable before 2030.

Meta preserves Clinton

Meta signed a 20-year deal with Constellation to preserve the Clinton Clean Energy Center in Illinois — a plant scheduled for decommissioning in 2027 — demonstrating that demand-side commitments can extend the life of existing clean assets.

The Road Ahead — Implications for AI Leaders

Microsoft has positioned itself as the first mover in a new model of corporate energy procurement — one that goes beyond certificates and directly funds the physical revival of clean baseload infrastructure. Competitors are already following, and the pace is accelerating.

For energy and technology professionals, the implications are clear: data centre siting will increasingly factor proximity to nuclear baseload, and long-term power purchase agreements will reshape capital allocation across the utility sector for decades to come. If the Crane Clean Energy Center restarts in 2027, it will mark the first time in US history that a decommissioned nuclear facility has returned to commercial service — a precedent that could unlock similar revivals at idled plants across the country.

Long-term, this may prove one of the most significant contributions of the AI era to decarbonisation: a virtuous cycle in which AI demand directly funds the clean energy infrastructure needed to sustain it responsibly.
